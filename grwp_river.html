The Genesee River: Promise, Problems, Need for New Approach

Genesee River Watershed with Counties and Riverside Multi-Use Trails



Hospital in Wellsville, NY, Hurricane Agnes flooding, 1972

The Genesee River begins in Potter County, PA, and flows northward over three spectacular falls in Letchworth State Park, NY. After exiting Mt. Morris Dam, it cascades over three more falls in Rochester before emptying into Lake Ontario.

Despite the Genesee's beauty, its water quality dramatically decreases over its 157-mile length. The Rochester Embayment was identified as one of 43 Great Lakes "Areas of Concern" in a 1987 agreement between the USA and Canada. Yet a study in 2012 concluded that the Genesee River still ranked 32nd among all rivers in the country in pounds of toxic materials discharged into its waters. Although much of this can be traced to companies in Rochester, the problems begin further upstream. Heavy metals and carcinogens such as benzene leaching from the Sinclair Oil Refinery and industrial park in Wellsville, NY, led to the site's registration on the national priority list for hazardous waste remediation in 1983.

The Genesee River also ranks as the greatest source of phosphorus among all New York State tributaries into Lake Ontario. This feeds algae blooms that endanger the lake's fishery and shoreline communities. Much of the phosphorus is from agricultural nutrients in Allegany County and other upstream areas. Despite the lessons of the Hurricane Agnes flood of 1972, encroachment of construction and agriculture up to the river's edge continues. This contributes to costly erosion and flooding that strains the budgets of the rural communities that permit these practices. Sediment deposited into the river by erosion imposes a further burden on these taxpayers by requiring dredging at Mt. Morris Dam and the Rochester harbor.

Before 2008, these problems were addressed on a small scale by the noble efforts of organizations such as Trout Unlimited and isolated investments by federal, state, and county agencies. Successes such as the completed remediation of the Sinclair Refinery in 2012 are offset by recent research quantifying the limited impact of previous work on phosphorus and sediment loads.
